\begin{abstract}
Modern cyber attacks are increasingly automated and adaptive, rendering
static rule-based defenses insufficient for real-time threat mitigation.
This paper proposes a closed-loop RL defense agent that formulates
packet-level security decision-making as a Markov Decision Process (MDP).
The agent observes network conditions through a 20-dimensional feature vector
combining network-behavioral features and HTTP payload semantic features,
the latter integrating OWASP CRS rules directly into the observation space
to capture application-layer attack patterns without requiring labeled traffic
for RL policy training. A Proximal Policy
Optimization (PPO) agent maps each observation to one of four hierarchical
defense actions: Allow, Rate Limit, Redirect (to honeypot), and Block.
We benchmark five defense strategies --- PPO-Tuned, PPO-Default, DQN, A2C,
and Rule-Based --- across five independent seeds. Domain-specific PPO hyperparameter tuning yields a \textbf{28\% variance
reduction} (std\,0.021 vs.\,0.029) and \textbf{false positive rate reduction
from 0.9\% to 0.7\%} compared to SB3 defaults, while both PPO variants
substantially outperform Rule-Based (reward\,=\,$-5.559$, FP\,=\,10.2\%). A2C collapses in 2/5 seeds (std\,=\,1.802), confirming that
PPO's on-policy stability with long rollouts is critical for closed-loop
IDS environments. Results demonstrate that in network security,
\emph{policy stability} and \emph{false positive minimization} are more
operationally relevant optimization targets than raw reward maximization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The rapid escalation of cyber threats targeting networked infrastructure
demands defense mechanisms capable of adapting to novel and evolving attack
patterns in real time. Traditional rule-based intrusion detection systems
such as Snort and Suricata operate on static signature databases, offering
low latency but no adaptation capability \cite{ring2019survey}. Machine
learning approaches using supervised classifiers have demonstrated high
accuracy on static benchmark datasets \cite{sharafaldin2018cicids}, yet
they treat defense as a one-shot classification problem --- decoupled from
the consequence of the defensive action itself.

Reinforcement learning (RL) offers a fundamentally different paradigm: the
defense agent interacts with the network environment, receives feedback
through a reward signal, and iteratively refines its policy based on the
long-term impact of its actions. This closed-loop property means that a
\textit{Block} action affects future observations --- attacker traffic
ceases --- while a \textit{Redirect} action preserves attack visibility
through a honeypot. Neither effect is capturable by a static classifier.

Applying RL to network defense introduces two non-trivial challenges.
First, the observation space must encode sufficient behavioral information
to distinguish multiple attack types simultaneously --- a requirement that
off-the-shelf flow feature extractors often fail to satisfy for Layer~7
attacks such as SQL Injection (SQLi) and Cross-Site Scripting (XSS), which
are indistinguishable at the network level. Second, general-purpose RL
implementations such as SB3 ship with default hyperparameters optimized for
standard benchmark environments (e.g., CartPole, MuJoCo) rather than
network security tasks.

This paper is guided by three research questions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{RQ1 (Comparative Effectiveness):} To what extent does a
    reinforcement learning-based agent outperform traditional rule-based
    mechanisms in mitigating automated and adaptive cyber attacks?

  \item \textbf{RQ2 (Operational Trade-off):} How does the implementation
    of autonomous defensive actions (blocking, rate limiting, and
    redirection) affect the balance between attack mitigation and
    legitimate service availability?

  \item \textbf{RQ3 (Policy Stability):} Can the RL-based agent develop
    and maintain a stable defense policy across diverse and evolving
    attack vectors without manual intervention?
\end{itemize}

This paper addresses these questions with the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A 20-dimensional observation module} combining bidirectional
    flow statistics (F1--F11) with HTTP payload semantic features (F12--F20),
    including OWASP CRS 941/942 rule scores embedded as continuous
    features --- a design not present in prior closed-loop RL defense systems,
    which operate on network-layer signals only \cite{hammar2024tnsm}.

  \item \textbf{A multi-layer payload normalization pipeline} performing
    recursive URL decoding, HTML entity decoding, Unicode NFKC normalization,
    and Base64 decoding, enabling real-time detection of double-encoded
    evasion payloads within the 1-second observation window.

  \item \textbf{A systematic hyperparameter sensitivity analysis} for PPO in
    the network defense domain, demonstrating that domain-aware tuning
    yields 28\% variance reduction and lower false positive rate over
    SB3 defaults, with a documented regression experiment isolating
    the contribution of each change.

  \item \textbf{A hierarchical four-action defense policy} with
    Redirect-to-honeypot as a strategic intermediate action, enabling
    threat intelligence collection while protecting production services.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning Fundamentals}

Reinforcement Learning (RL) is a branch of machine learning that enables
agents to learn optimal behaviors through interaction with an environment.
Unlike supervised learning, which relies on labeled datasets, RL allows an
agent to learn directly from experience by receiving feedback in the form
of rewards or penalties \cite{sutton2018rl}. This learning paradigm is
particularly suitable for dynamic and adversarial domains such as
cybersecurity, where labeled data is scarce and attack strategies evolve
continuously.

\subsubsection{RL Framework}
The reinforcement learning process is formally modeled as a Markov Decision
Process (MDP), defined by a tuple $(S, A, P, R, \gamma)$, where: $S$ is
the set of possible states; $A$ is the set of available actions;
$P(s'|s,a)$ is the state-transition probability; $R(s,a)$ is the reward
function; and $\gamma \in [0,1]$ is the discount factor balancing immediate
and future rewards. The objective is to learn a policy $\pi(a|s)$ that
maximizes the expected cumulative reward. In network defense, this
corresponds to learning mitigation action sequences that reduce attack
impact while maintaining acceptable system performance.

\subsubsection{Model-Free vs.\ Model-Based RL}
RL algorithms are broadly categorized into model-based and model-free
approaches. Model-based methods attempt to learn or utilize an explicit
environmental dynamics model for planning. While sample-efficient, accurately
modeling complex network environments and attacker behaviors is often
impractical. Model-free RL, by contrast, learns optimal policies directly
from interactions without an explicit model, making it more commonly
applied in cybersecurity research due to its flexibility in handling
complex, partially observable environments \cite{nguyen2021drl}.

\subsubsection{Deep Reinforcement Learning}
Traditional RL techniques struggle to scale to environments with large or
continuous state spaces. Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) addresses this
by integrating deep neural networks as function approximators for value
functions or policies. Value-based DRL methods such as Deep Q-Networks
(DQN) \cite{mnih2015dqn} approximate the action-value function using
neural networks. Policy-based methods directly optimize the policy function
and are better suited for environments with high-dimensional action spaces.
Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) \cite{schulman2017ppo} has gained
widespread adoption due to its training stability: PPO constrains policy
updates to prevent overly large changes, a property critical for network
defense where unstable policies may trigger disruptive or overly aggressive
mitigation actions.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Network Simulation Environments}

Effective training and evaluation of RL agents in cybersecurity require
controlled, reproducible, and observable environments. Deploying learning
agents directly in production networks poses significant risks, including
service disruption and unintended security violations. Network simulation
environments have therefore become an essential research tool
\cite{peuster2016containernet}.

\subsubsection{Role of Simulation in Cybersecurity Research}
Network simulations enable researchers to model complex infrastructures,
generate realistic attack traffic, and observe system behavior under
adversarial conditions. Unlike static datasets, simulated environments
support interactive learning, where an agent's actions directly influence
future network states --- a property that aligns naturally with RL's
continuous agent--environment interaction loop. Simulation also allows the
safe execution of destructive attack scenarios such as DDoS, brute-force
authentication attacks, and reconnaissance scanning without ethical or
operational concerns, and facilitates repeatable experiments enabling fair
comparisons between different defense policies.

\subsubsection{Mininet for Network Emulation}
Mininet is a widely adopted network emulation framework that creates virtual
networks using lightweight Linux containers \cite{lantz2010mininet}. It
leverages the Linux kernel's networking stack to provide realistic modeling
of latency, bandwidth, and packet loss, making it suitable for simulating
enterprise-scale topologies including routers, switches, servers, and end
hosts. In cybersecurity research, Mininet has been extensively used to study
intrusion detection systems, traffic analysis, and automated defense
mechanisms. Its ability to dynamically reconfigure network topologies at
runtime enables simulation of evolving attack surfaces. Mininet integrates
seamlessly with security tools such as Iptables and TC (Traffic Control),
enabling low-level enforcement of defensive actions.

\subsubsection{Reinforcement Learning Environments with Gymnasium}
Gymnasium \cite{towers2023gymnasium}, the successor of OpenAI Gym, provides
a standardized interface for RL environments, defining clear abstractions
for state spaces, action spaces, reward functions, and episode termination
conditions. In network defense, Gymnasium bridges the RL agent and the
simulated network: metrics such as traffic volume, connection rates, and
alert signals are encoded into numerical state representations, while
defensive actions such as IP blocking, rate limiting, or traffic redirection
are mapped into discrete action spaces. The use of Gymnasium ensures
compatibility with widely adopted RL libraries including Stable-Baselines3
\cite{raffin2021sb3}, enabling rapid experimentation with modern DRL
algorithms while maintaining reproducibility.

\subsubsection{Hybrid Simulation Architectures}
Recent research increasingly adopts hybrid simulation architectures that
combine network emulation platforms with RL frameworks. In such
architectures, Mininet generates realistic network behavior while Gymnasium
manages the RL interaction loop. This separation of concerns enables
flexible experimentation. Hybrid environments also support the integration
of monitoring and visualization tools, such as SIEM systems, by feeding
simulated logs into security platforms so researchers can observe how
autonomous agents influence security metrics over time. This approach
closely resembles real-world operational settings, enhancing the practical
relevance of experimental results \cite{peuster2016containernet}.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Automated Incident Response Mechanisms}

As cyber attacks become increasingly fast, scalable, and automated,
traditional manual incident response processes are no longer sufficient.
Security Operation Centers (SOCs) often face delayed response times due to
alert overload, human error, and limited situational awareness.
Automated Incident Response (AIR) mechanisms aim to enable rapid, consistent,
and adaptive defensive actions.

\subsubsection{From Detection to Response Automation}
Conventional security systems primarily focus on detection, leaving response
decisions to human operators. While Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) can
identify suspicious activities, the lack of automated enforcement allows
attackers to continue exploiting systems during response delays. Automation
enables immediate execution of predefined or adaptive actions once malicious
behavior is identified --- including blocking malicious IP addresses,
throttling abnormal traffic, isolating compromised hosts, or redirecting
attackers to deception environments. By reducing reliance on manual
intervention, automated response systems significantly improve reaction
speed and operational consistency \cite{ring2019survey}.

\subsubsection{Rule-Based and Policy-Based Response Systems}
Early automated response systems relied on static rules and predefined
security policies. For example, if a connection threshold is exceeded, an
IP address may be automatically blacklisted. While effective against known
attack patterns, such rule-based approaches struggle to handle evolving
threats and often generate false positives. Policy-based systems attempt to
improve flexibility by defining higher-level response objectives; however,
they still depend heavily on expert-defined policies and lack the ability
to learn from environmental feedback. As attack strategies become more
adaptive, static response logic increasingly fails to maintain an optimal
balance between security and service availability.

\subsubsection{RL for Adaptive Incident Response}
RL introduces a paradigm shift in automated incident response by enabling
agents to learn optimal defense strategies through environment interaction
\cite{malialis2015ddos,nguyen2021drl}. Instead of relying on fixed rules,
RL agents evaluate the consequences of their actions using reward signals
derived from security and performance metrics, allowing the system to
dynamically adapt response strategies to different attack intensities and
patterns. Hammar and Stadler \cite{hammar2024tnsm} formulate the
defender--attacker interaction as an optimal stopping game in simulated IT
infrastructure, demonstrating that RL-based response systems can outperform
static approaches in handling complex multi-stage attacks.

\subsubsection{Low-Level Enforcement Mechanisms}
Effective automated response requires reliable enforcement at the network and
system levels. Linux kernel-level tools such as Iptables and Traffic Control
(TC) are commonly used to implement real-time defensive actions: Iptables
enables fine-grained packet filtering and connection blocking, while TC
allows dynamic traffic shaping and rate limiting. These mechanisms operate
at low level, ensuring minimal latency and high enforcement accuracy. By
integrating RL decision-making with kernel-level controls, automated defense
systems can respond to attacks with both intelligence and speed, bridging
the gap between high-level AI reasoning and practical network security
enforcement.

\subsubsection{Deception-Based Response and Honeypots}
Honeypots are decoy systems designed to attract attackers and observe
malicious behavior without risking production assets. By redirecting
suspicious traffic to honeypots, defenders can gather valuable intelligence
while reducing direct attack impact. Automated redirection mechanisms allow
deception to be applied dynamically, based on real-time threat assessments.
When combined with RL, deception strategies can be selectively activated
to maximize intelligence gain while minimizing system disruption, enhancing
both defensive effectiveness and situational awareness.

\subsubsection{Integration with SIEM Systems}
Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) systems aggregate and
correlate security logs from multiple sources to provide centralized
visibility. Integrating automated response mechanisms with SIEM platforms
allows administrators to monitor defensive actions and system performance in
real time. Visualization and alert correlation improve trust in autonomous
systems by providing transparency and auditability. In research environments,
SIEM integration enables quantitative evaluation of response effectiveness
through metrics such as alert reduction, response latency, and attack
mitigation success --- critical for validating autonomous defense agents and
assessing their readiness for real-world deployment.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Feature Engineering for Network Traffic}

Feature engineering plays a critical role in applying ML and RL techniques
to network security. Raw network traffic --- consisting of packets and flows
--- is inherently high-dimensional, noisy, and heterogeneous. Without
appropriate feature representation, learning agents may struggle to
distinguish between benign and malicious behaviors \cite{ring2019survey}.

\subsubsection{Packet-Level and Flow-Level Features}
Network traffic features are broadly categorized into packet-level and
flow-level representations. Packet-level features include source/destination
IP addresses, protocol types, packet sizes, and TCP flags; while they
provide fine-grained visibility, they impose high computational overhead and
limited temporal context. Flow-level features aggregate packets sharing
common characteristics within a time window: common attributes include
connection duration, packet count, byte counts, average packet size, and
inter-arrival times \cite{sharafaldin2018cicids,moustafa2015unswnb15}.
Flow-based representations reduce dimensionality and better capture
behavioral patterns, making them more suitable for real-time analysis.

\subsubsection{Statistical and Behavioral Features}
Beyond basic header information, statistical features provide insights
into traffic dynamics: packet rate, connection frequency, source/destination
address entropy, and variance of packet sizes are commonly used and are
effective in detecting volumetric attacks such as DoS/DDoS. Behavioral
features capture deviations from normal traffic patterns over time, such
as sudden spikes in connection attempts, abnormal protocol usage, and
repeated authentication failures --- particularly valuable for identifying
reconnaissance activities and brute-force attacks.

\subsubsection{Feature Extraction Tools}
Automated feature extraction tools are essential for real-time network
monitoring. Scapy \cite{biondi2022scapy} is a widely used Python-based
framework for packet capture and protocol analysis, enabling flexible
parsing of packet headers and payloads and supporting custom feature
extraction pipelines. In simulated environments, packet capture can be
combined with flow aggregation techniques to generate structured feature
vectors suitable as inputs for RL agents.
CICFlowMeter \cite{sharafaldin2018cicids} provides over 80 bidirectional
flow statistical features, achieving ${>}97\%$ accuracy on CICIDS2017 with
Random Forest. Moustafa and Slay \cite{moustafa2015unswnb15} reported
85.6\% accuracy on UNSW-NB15 with 0.89\% FPR for binary classification.
While effective for network-level attacks, these approaches lack semantic
content features for Layer~7 attacks (SQLi, XSS).

\subsubsection{State Representation for Reinforcement Learning}
In RL-based defense systems, feature engineering directly shapes the state
space design. The state representation must balance informativeness with
computational efficiency: overly complex states slow training and cause
instability, while overly simplistic states omit critical security signals.
Typical state vectors include normalized traffic statistics, alert counts,
and resource utilization metrics. Palmer et al.\ \cite{palmer2023drlsurvey}
identify high-dimensional observation spaces and multi-attack generalization
as the primary open challenges for DRL-based cyber defense.

\subsubsection{Challenges in Network Feature Engineering}
Several challenges arise in network traffic feature engineering. Encrypted
traffic limits visibility into payload contents, requiring reliance on
metadata and statistical features. Network behavior is highly dynamic,
leading to concept drift that can degrade model performance over time.
Feature selection and normalization present additional difficulty: redundant
or highly correlated features may negatively impact learning efficiency,
requiring careful design and continuous evaluation to maintain robust and
adaptive defense performance. Deep learning methods including CNN and LSTM
architectures have improved detection on imbalanced attack datasets
\cite{ring2019survey}, yet they remain passive classifiers --- once a
detection decision is made, no action is taken and no feedback is received,
and none close the loop between detection and response.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Summary and Research Gap}

This review reveals that traditional network defense mechanisms --- rule-based
firewalls, signature-based IDS --- remain effective against known attack
patterns but struggle to adapt to novel and automated threats. Advances in
RL demonstrate strong potential for adaptive and autonomous network defense,
and simulation environments (Mininet + Gymnasium) have become the standard
backbone for developing and validating autonomous defense agents.

However, several research gaps persist. First, most existing RL-based
approaches focus on network-layer features only, leaving Layer~7 attacks
(SQLi, XSS) unaddressed because application-layer attack payloads are
indistinguishable at the network level without payload inspection. Second,
prior RL-IDS works rely on default hyperparameter settings without analyzing
policy stability across seeds --- a gap that limits deployment confidence.
Third, the integration of security domain knowledge (WAF/CRS rules) directly
into the RL observation space has not been explored: the OWASP ModSecurity
CRS \cite{owaspCRS2023} provides community-validated rules for detecting
SQLi and XSS, but prior work treats WAF rules and RL agents as separate
systems. Finally, few works combine closed-loop RL decision-making with
low-level kernel enforcement mechanisms (Iptables, TC) and deception
strategies (honeypot redirection) within a single integrated system.

This paper addresses these gaps with: (1) a 20-dimensional observation
module combining network-behavioral features (F1--F11) with HTTP payload
semantic features (F12--F20) including embedded OWASP CRS 941/942 rule
scores as continuous RL observation features --- the first such integration
in a closed-loop RL defense system; (2) a systematic five-algorithm,
five-seed hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for PPO in the network defense
domain; and (3) a hierarchical four-action defense policy incorporating
honeypot redirection as a dynamic deception strategy.

\section{System Architecture and MDP Formulation}

\subsection{System Overview}
The proposed defense agent operates as a closed-loop three-layer architecture
deployed at the network edge:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Observation Layer:} Captures raw packets via Scapy (L3/L4)
    and tshark (HTTPS via SSLKEYLOGFILE), organizes them into bidirectional
    5-tuple flows, and computes a 20-dimensional feature vector every 1
    second.
  \item \textbf{Decision Layer:} A PPO agent with MLP policy receives the
    normalized observation vector and selects a defense action.
  \item \textbf{Execution Layer:} Translates actions into Iptables rules
    applied to the router Network Namespace via \texttt{nsenter}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{MDP Formulation}

\textbf{State Space} $\mathcal{S} \subset [0,1]^{20}$: Each state is a
20-dimensional vector representing network behavior in a 1-second sliding
window. Normalization to $[0,1]$ ensures gradient descent stability in the
PPO neural network.

\textbf{Action Space} $\mathcal{A} = \{0,1,2,3\}$: Four discrete defense
actions ordered by escalating intervention intensity
(Allow\,$<$\,Rate\,Limit\,$<$\,Redirect\,$<$\,Block), enabling the agent
to apply the minimum sufficient response while preserving service
availability:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textit{Allow} (0): No intervention.
  \item \textit{Rate Limit} (1): Iptables hashlimit 10/sec burst 20.
  \item \textit{Redirect} (2): NAT to honeypot port 4443. Escalates to
    Block after 60\,s if L7 signals persist.
  \item \textit{Block} (3): Iptables DROP --- for volumetric attacks.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Reward Function:}
\begin{equation}
  R_t = -\!\left(D_{\text{after}} + C_{\text{action}}\right)
        + B_{\text{reduction}} + B_{\text{action}}
  \label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
where $D \in [0,1]$ is a weighted sum of six damage components reflecting
operational impact severity.
Weights are assigned based on potential service disruption: volumetric threats (DDoS, L7 attacks)
receive the highest weight (0.25 each) due to their potential for complete
service disruption; scanning and connection-failure indicators receive
moderate weight (0.15 each) as they represent active reconnaissance
preceding full exploitation; payload and SYN anomalies receive lower
weight (0.10 each) as standalone indicators they rarely cause direct damage.
Non-linear activations (logistic for unbounded intensity metrics;
tanh for ratio-bounded metrics) compress outlier values into $[0,1]$
without hard clipping:

\begin{center}\scriptsize
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{3pt}
\begin{tabular}{llccl}
\toprule
\textbf{Component} & \textbf{Feature} & \textbf{Weight} & \textbf{Fn.} & \textbf{Rationale} \\
\midrule
DDoS overflow      & F1 (PacketRate)    & 0.25 & logistic & Service disruption \\
SYN flood          & F2 (SynAckRatio)   & 0.10 & tanh     & Ratio-bounded \\
Connection failure & F4 (RstRatio)      & 0.15 & tanh     & Recon indicator \\
Port scan spread   & F5 (DistinctPorts) & 0.15 & tanh     & Recon indicator \\
Payload anomaly    & F9 (AvgPayload)    & 0.10 & logistic & Standalone signal \\
SQLi/XSS (L7)      & F13,F14,F18,F19   & 0.25 & weighted & Application breach \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

$C$ is an action cost ladder (Allow\,=\,0, RateLimit\,=\,0.02,
Redirect\,=\,0.05, Block\,=\,0.08), $B_{\text{action}}$ is a
soft-gating detection bonus (+0.25 correct / $-$0.40 to $-$0.60
mismatches), and $B_{\text{reduction}} = (\text{damage\_before} -
\text{damage\_after}) \times 0.5$.

\textbf{Discount Factor} $\gamma = 0.99$: Encourages long-term damage
minimization over 120-step episodes.

\textbf{Source-scoped HTTP IAT Uniformity (F7):} All features except F7
are computed per bidirectional flow. F7 is the only \emph{source-IP-scoped}
feature: brute-force tools (Hydra, Burp Suite Intruder) open a new TCP
connection per request, so per-flow IAT contains only one sample and
yields no timing signal. F7 aggregates HTTP timestamps across \emph{all
flows from the same source IP} within the 1-second window, enabling
detection of inter-request timing regularity characteristic of automation
regardless of per-connection boundaries.

\textbf{OWASP CRS integration (F13, F18):} Rather than building L7 detection
rules from scratch, F13 and F18 embed OWASP CRS~942 (SQLi, 50 rules, PL2)
and CRS~941 (XSS, 27 regex\,+\,9 phrase rules, PL2) scores as continuous
features. On the LSNM2024 URI dataset ($n{=}2{,}809$ SQLi\,+\,3{,}000
normal) \cite{lashkari2024lsnm}, CRS PL2 achieves $\text{F1}{=}0.925$,
Recall\,=\,1.0 with FPR\,=\,15.3\% on the CRS-only test. In the
closed-loop RL system, the agent learns to weight F13/F18 signals
alongside network-layer features, reducing effective false positives
to 0.7\%.

\textbf{Payload normalization pipeline:} Prior to F12--F20 computation, each
HTTP payload passes through: HTML entity decoding $\rightarrow$ Unicode NFKC
normalization $\rightarrow$ recursive URL decoding (max 2 iterations,
handling double-encoding bypass \texttt{\%2527}$\rightarrow$\texttt{\%27}$\rightarrow$\texttt{'}) $\rightarrow$ recursive Base64 decoding (max 2 iterations)
$\rightarrow$ whitespace collapse $\rightarrow$ lowercase.
Benchmarked on 5,000 mixed-payload calls: mean\,=\,32.4\,µs/call,
P99\,=\,235.6\,µs, throughput\,=\,30,916\,calls/sec --- sufficient for
real-time processing within the 1-second observation window.

\section{Experimental Results}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Environment:} Linux, Containernet (Mininet\,+\,Docker)
\cite{peuster2016containernet}, Python~3.x, Stable-Baselines3
\cite{raffin2021sb3}, NumPy\,$<$\,2.0.

\textbf{Datasets:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textit{Training:} \texttt{IDSDefenseEnv} \cite{towers2023gymnasium},
    a custom Gym-compatible environment simulating five synthetic IP types
    (benign, SYN flood, port scan, brute force, SQLi/XSS) with per-type
    behavioral distributions and ${\pm}20\%$ domain randomization ---
    500,000 timesteps.
  \item \textit{CRS calibration:} LSNM2024 \cite{lashkari2024lsnm} (SQLi
    and XSS subsets) --- 3,000 normal\,+\,2,809 labeled SQLi URIs for
    CRS-942 PL selection; 3,000 normal\,+\,123 labeled XSS URIs for
    CRS-941 PL selection.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Benchmark Methodology.}
All learning-based methods are evaluated on the shared \texttt{IDSDefenseEnv}
under an identical MDP, following the canonical deep~RL
benchmarking paradigm \cite{mnih2015dqn,towers2023gymnasium}. All methods
are trained under the same interaction budget (500k timesteps), evaluated
with the same protocol (mean episodic return), and repeated across five
random seeds. Henderson et al.\ \cite{henderson2018deeprl} demonstrate that
RL results are highly sensitive to random initialization, hyperparameters,
and implementation details; we therefore report mean $\pm$ standard
deviation and 95\% confidence intervals.
This is analogous to standard deep RL practice of comparing algorithms on a
shared environment under identical reward and observation interfaces~\cite{mnih2015dqn}.
Following SB3 best practices \cite{raffin2021sb3}, all preprocessing,
normalization, and reward handling are fully documented, as implementation
details can significantly affect RL benchmark results \cite{engstrom2020}.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Three Tiers of Contribution}

\textbf{Tier 1 --- RL vs.\ Rule-Based:} All RL algorithms achieve positive
mean reward, while Rule-Based yields a large negative reward with
FP\,=\,10.2\% --- over 11$\times$ higher than any RL method (Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}).
Fixed thresholds cannot adapt when traffic baselines shift.
Notably, DQN achieves the highest raw reward but lower detection rate than
PPO variants, indicating it learns a reward-maximizing strategy that favors
Allow on ambiguous traffic rather than accurate attack classification.

\textbf{Tier 2 --- PPO vs.\ A2C:} Both PPO variants converge reliably across
all 5~seeds. A2C collapses in 2/5~seeds, a production-unacceptable failure
rate. Root cause: A2C's short rollout (\texttt{n\_steps\,=\,5}) produces
inaccurate advantages in the IDS environment, where a Block action suppresses
attacker traffic for several steps before the next attack arrives. PPO's long
rollout covers the full suppress-return cycle \cite{colas2019seeds}.

\textbf{Tier 3 --- PPO-Tuned vs.\ PPO-Default:} Tuning gains are
distributional rather than mean-level (Table~\ref{tab:tuning}). In security
operations, a consistently reliable policy outperforms a higher-average policy
with unpredictable seed-to-seed variation \cite{henderson2018deeprl}.
Cohen's $d{=}{-1.290}$ confirms large practical effect on stability.

\subsection{Why Each Hyperparameter Change Matters}

\textbf{net\_arch} [64,\,64]\,$\rightarrow$\,[128,\,128]: The 20D
observation spans two semantically different domains --- flow statistics
(F1--F11) and payload attack signals (F12--F20) at heterogeneous scales.
A [64,\,64] network cannot maintain independent decision boundaries for
five attack subspaces simultaneously.

\textbf{LR Schedule} $3{\to}1{\times}10^{-4}$: Two-phase optimization:
high LR for rapid exploration of coarse attack signatures, then low LR
for fine-grained L7 calibration. V3 experiment confirms that a fixed
high LR causes late-training policy oscillation.

\textbf{ent\_coef} $0.0{\to}0.005$: Prevents policy collapse to a dominant
strategy (e.g., always Block) without over-suppressing reward signals.
Smaller than the common 0.01 to preserve reward-driven convergence. The
V3 regression (ent\_coef\,=\,0.03) confirms the threshold effect.

\textbf{vf\_coef} $0.5{\to}1.0$: In the IDS closed-loop, an attacker
who is Blocked goes silent for 5--11 steps. The critic must learn that
silence after Block is a valid continuation state. Equal weighting of
critic and actor losses forces the value network to learn post-Block
dynamics at the same rate as the policy, reducing policy gradient
variance \cite{engstrom2020}.

\subsection{OWASP CRS as RL Observation Features}

Embedding CRS scores (F13, F18) as continuous observation features
represents a knowledge transfer mechanism: decades of security community
expertise encoded in 77 rules (50 SQLi\,+\,27 XSS) are compressed into
two scalar signals that the RL agent can act upon without requiring labeled
L7 training data. This is particularly valuable given the scarcity of
labeled HTTP attack traffic in public datasets.

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Stochasticity and reproducibility.} RL benchmarks are stochastic
and high-variance; a single run is insufficient to claim superiority
\cite{henderson2018deeprl}. Results are task-specific to \texttt{IDSDefenseEnv}
rather than universally generalizable \cite{colas2019seeds,engstrom2020}.
The $p$-value of 0.0757 does not cross $\alpha{=}0.05$ at $n{=}5$; we
recommend $n{\geq}20$ seeds for stronger statistical confirmation.

\textbf{Operational constraints.} \texttt{IDSDefenseEnv} uses synthetic
per-IP behavioral distributions with ${\pm}20\%$ domain randomization. The 1,016\,ms end-to-end cycle
cannot respond to the first second of a flash SYN Flood; a static
rate-limiter at the router is recommended. F12--F20 require decrypted
HTTP payload (SSLKEYLOGFILE) in HTTPS environments.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper presented a closed-loop RL defense agent using PPO with a
20-dimensional observation module combining network behavioral features and
OWASP CRS-based payload semantics. Five defense strategies were benchmarked
across five independent seeds, and the PPO-Tuned policy was evaluated against
conventional detection baselines.

Three findings emerge: (1) RL-based defense universally outperforms the
rule-based baseline, reducing FP Rate by over 11$\times$; (2) PPO's
on-policy long-rollout architecture is essential for closed-loop IDS
stability --- A2C collapses in 40\% of runs due to short advantage
estimation; (3) domain-aware hyperparameter tuning reduces policy variance
by 28\% and FP Rate, with Cohen's $d{=}{-1.290}$, gains that are
operationally more significant than raw reward differences.

Future work includes multi-agent SDN extension, adversarial robustness
evaluation, and sim-to-real transfer validation on live network traffic.
